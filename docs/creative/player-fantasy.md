# ChronoQueue — Player Fantasy Document

This document defines what the player is supposed to *feel*, not just what they *do*. Every major feature must connect to one of these fantasy statements.

---

## Primary Fantasy

> **The player feels like the amused keeper of a chaotic adventuring party** when they **check in on their heroes' ridiculous exploits** because **the game generates a constant stream of surprising, funny, and occasionally epic moments that happened without them.**

This is the emotional core. The player is not the hero — they're the one watching the heroes. Think of it as the difference between playing D&D and reading a recap of someone else's chaotic campaign. The joy is in discovery: "What did my idiots get up to while I was gone?"

Key emotions: amusement, surprise, mild pride, affectionate exasperation.

---

## Secondary Fantasy

> **The player feels like a systems tinkerer who's in on the joke** when they **optimize builds and loadouts for heroes who don't know they're being min-maxed** because **the real game reveals itself as a satisfying optimization puzzle wrapped in absurdist comedy.**

This fantasy activates when the player moves from passive watching to active engagement. The gap between the silly fiction ("Gerald the Knight carries 47 swords") and the real strategic depth ("stacking fire resistance makes Gerald's build viable for the Inferno Zones") is where the magic lives.

Key emotions: cleverness, satisfaction, mastery, conspiratorial fun.

---

## Tertiary Fantasy

> **The player feels like someone rediscovering a favorite book** when they **return after hours or days away** because **there's always a backlog of adventures, loot, and stories waiting — never a punishment for leaving.**

This fantasy sustains long-term retention. Every return is a gift, not a chore. The log is full of things that happened. Some are funny. Some are surprising. Occasionally, something genuinely cool happened — a rare drop, a boss kill, a ridiculous sequence of events.

Key emotions: pleasant surprise, comfort, low-key excitement, "oh, nice."

---

## Anti-Fantasies (What the Player Should Never Feel)

| Feeling | Cause | Pillar Violation |
|---------|-------|------------------|
| Guilt for not playing | Missed events, decay mechanics, timed rewards | Pillar 1: Zero-Pressure Play |
| Boredom from repetition | Same log entries, no variety in outcomes | Pillar 3: Joy of Watching |
| Cringe at the writing | Forced humor, "lol random" jokes, try-hard comedy | Pillar 2: Parody With Substance |
| Confusion about what to do | Unclear progression, unexplained systems | Pillar 4: Elegant Text |
| Contempt for the game's depth | Systems that are *only* jokes with no real mechanics | Pillar 2: Parody With Substance |

---

## Fantasy Validation Protocol

For every proposed feature, the Game Designer must answer:

1. Which fantasy does this serve? (Primary, Secondary, or Tertiary)
2. Write a concrete scenario: "The player opens the game and sees [X]. They feel [Y] because [Z]."
3. Does this scenario trigger any anti-fantasy? If yes, redesign.

If a feature cannot produce a convincing fantasy scenario, its purpose is unclear. Push back with questions before building it.
